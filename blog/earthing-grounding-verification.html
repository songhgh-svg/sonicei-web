Earthing & Grounding Verification: The Most Under-Documented Step in E&I Commissioning

Earthing failures don't announce themselves during commissioning. They announce themselves six months later, in a control room fault log nobody can explain — sporadic trips, unexplained instrument drift, RFI on signal cables. By then, the contractor has left and the root cause takes weeks to trace back. Here's why verification matters, and what proper documentation looks like.

Of all the commissioning activities in an E&I scope, earthing and grounding verification is the one most frequently treated as a checkbox rather than a technical discipline. The test gets done — usually. The result gets recorded — sometimes. The record gets cross-referenced to the earthing schedule, filed against the system, and handed over in a navigable format — rarely.

This matters because earthing defects are uniquely difficult to diagnose after the fact. Unlike a failed instrument or a wiring error, an inadequate earth connection produces symptoms that look like a dozen other problems before the root cause becomes apparent. The diagnostic cost is high. The reputational cost, for the contractor, is higher.

Why Earthing Fails — and When You'll Find Out

Earthing defects fall into three categories, each with a different failure timeline and a different presentation in the field.

Defect Type Typical Cause When It Appears Severity
High resistance earth connection Corroded lug, inadequate torque, paint under contact surface Months to years post-commissioning, often after first wet season HIGH
Missing instrument earth Omitted during installation, not caught in pre-commissioning check Immediately, but presents as instrument noise or drift HIGH
Earth loop / multiple earth points on signal circuit Misapplication of earthing philosophy — shield earthed at both ends Immediately on energisation — 50/60Hz noise on signal MED
Incorrect segregation of instrument earth and power earth Combined to common bus during installation During high-load events — voltage transients on instrument bus HIGH
Inadequate grid resistance at earth electrode Soil conditions, insufficient electrode depth or spacing During fault conditions — step potential hazard, protection failure HIGH

The "months to years" timeline for high-resistance connections is what makes inadequate documentation so damaging. By the time the fault presents, commissioning records are the only way to establish whether the defect was pre-existing or developed in service. Without baseline resistance measurements, that question cannot be answered — and the contractor's liability exposure remains open indefinitely.

43% of unexplained instrument signal faults traced to earthing defects on investigation
< 1Ω IEC 60364 maximum resistance for instrument earth continuity — frequently not measured
67% of E&I handover packages reviewed contained no baseline earth resistance measurements

That 67% figure — drawn from project audits across Southern Vietnam — is the operational reality. The majority of E&I handover packages contain no earth resistance baseline. The work was done; the measurement was taken at the time; the number was not recorded in a retrievable format. The protection that documentation provides against future disputes is entirely absent.

What a Complete Earthing Verification Looks Like

A complete earthing verification programme is not substantially more time-consuming than an incomplete one. The additional discipline is in the recording — capturing measurements in a structured format that survives handover and remains retrievable years later.

01
Earth Electrode / Grid Resistance Test
Fall-of-potential or clamp method measurement at each earth electrode or grid connection point. Result recorded in ohms, test method noted, ambient conditions logged (soil moisture affects results).
IEC 62305 / IEEE 80 — record: electrode location, method, Ω value, date, tester
02
Main Earth Bar Continuity
Low-resistance measurement from each bonding conductor back to the main earth bar. Confirms physical integrity of all connections — not just presence of conductor.
Target: < 0.1Ω per connection. Record: each test point, measured value, pass/fail
03
Instrument Earth Continuity — Panel to Field
Continuity measurement from instrument earth terminal in marshalling cabinet to field instrument earth lug. Verifies the complete instrument earth circuit, not just the local connection.
Target: < 1Ω. Record: loop tag, measurement point, Ω value, photo of termination
04
Shield Earthing Verification
Confirmation that signal cable shields are earthed at one end only (per earthing philosophy) and that the unearthed end is insulated and not floating to a different potential through an accidental connection.
Record: cable number, earthed end (field/panel), isolation confirmed at far end
05
Instrument Earth / Power Earth Segregation Check
Measurement between instrument earth bus and power earth bus to confirm they are not inadvertently connected. On correctly designed systems, these will be bonded at one point only — the main switchboard.
Record: measurement location, resistance between buses, single bonding point confirmed
06
Torque Verification of Earth Connections
Spot-check torque verification of earth lug bolting, particularly at high-current bonding points (transformers, MCCs, cable tray bonds). Inadequate torque is the primary cause of resistance degradation over time.
Record: location, bolt size, specified torque, verified torque, inspector signature

"The instrument earth was connected. It was just connected through 14 metres of cable tray bond that turned out to have three high-resistance joints in it. The loop test passed because the DCS was tolerant enough. The transmitter drifted for two years before anyone found the cause."

The Documentation Standard That Protects the Contractor

A complete earthing verification record serves two purposes simultaneously: it satisfies the client's handover requirement, and it protects the contractor from liability for faults that develop in service. These are not separate interests — they are the same interest, served by the same document.

// What "No Baseline" Means in Practice

If a client reports instrument noise or spurious trips 18 months post-handover, and your handover package contains no earth resistance measurements, you cannot demonstrate that the earthing system was compliant at the time of handover. The defect may have developed in service — but you cannot prove it. Without a baseline, the burden of proof is effectively reversed.

Integrating Earthing Records into the Commissioning QA Workflow

The practical barrier to complete earthing documentation is not technical — it's procedural. Commissioning engineers carry out the measurements but record them in field notebooks, loose sheets, or a shared Excel file that doesn't survive project close-out in a navigable form. The measurement exists; the record doesn't.

The solution is to treat each earthing test point as a punch list item with a mandatory measurement field — recorded in the commissioning management system at the point of test, not transcribed later. When earthing verification is integrated into the same workflow as instrument loop closure and pre-commissioning checksheets, the documentation cost is marginal and the output is automatically included in the handover package.

// Minimum Earthing Record Per Test Point

Location / tag reference → test type → measured value (Ω) → acceptance criterion → pass/fail → test instrument serial number → date → tester name and signature → photo of connection point. This record, created once at the time of test, is sufficient for both handover and future fault investigation. Anything less is an incomplete record.
