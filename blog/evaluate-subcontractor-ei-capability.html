How to Evaluate a Subcontractor's E&I Capability Before They're On Site

The wrong subcontractor doesn't fail on day one. They fail on day 40, when rework is expensive, the schedule has no float left, and replacing them is operationally impossible. The evaluation happens before mobilisation — or it doesn't happen at all.

Subcontractor selection in E&I commissioning is one of the highest-leverage decisions a project manager makes — and one of the least systematically evaluated. Most selection processes rely on three inputs: price, a list of previous projects, and someone's personal familiarity with the company. Two of those three inputs are poor predictors of on-site performance.

Price tells you about commercial position, not technical capability. A project reference list tells you the subcontractor was present on a project — not that they performed well, not that their defect rate was acceptable, not that they delivered documentation that survived handover. The third input — personal familiarity — is the most reliable, and the least scalable.

What follows is a structured evaluation framework built from field experience with E&I subcontractors across offshore, power plant, and industrial commissioning in Vietnam. It is not a tender scoring matrix. It is a capability diagnostic — designed to surface the information that actually predicts performance.

The Signals That Predict Performance — and the Ones That Don't

Before the framework, it's worth being explicit about which evaluation inputs carry real predictive weight and which are largely noise in an E&I context.

✗ Low Signal — Often Misleading
✓ High Signal — Actually Predictive
ISO 9001 certificate without current audit evidence
Defect rate on previous comparable project — verified with client reference
Long project reference list without performance data
Sample ITR / inspection records from a previous project
Headcount or company size
Ratio of qualified E&I technicians to unskilled labour in proposed crew
Tender price significantly below market
How they respond to a technical scenario question about a specific failure mode
Glossy capability brochure
Their proposed QA/QC plan — specificity and ownership, not length
Years in business
Supervisor's personal track record — not the company's

The distinction matters because a subcontractor with strong marketing materials and a weak site supervisor will consistently underperform against a smaller company with an experienced, disciplined field lead. E&I commissioning quality is delivered by people, not organisations — and the evaluation should reflect that.

71% of subcontractor underperformance events traced to supervisor capability, not company capability
3× higher defect rate from crews where QA/QC responsibility was not assigned to a named individual
58% of subcontractor selections made primarily on price — the single strongest predictor of rework events

The Evaluation Framework: Five Categories

Each category below addresses a distinct dimension of E&I subcontractor capability. The questions are designed to be asked directly — in a pre-award technical interview, not just assessed from tender documentation. How a subcontractor answers matters as much as what they answer.

01 Technical Competence of Key Personnel Highest Weight
Who is the proposed site supervisor, and what is their personal commissioning history — specifically on projects of comparable scope and complexity? Ask for CV, verify independently
What is the proposed ratio of qualified E&I technicians (with verifiable certification) to general labour in the mobilisation plan?
Describe how your team would approach loop testing on a 4–20mA HART transmitter where the DCS is showing a process value 15% above the calibrated output. Walk me through the diagnostic steps. Red flag if answer is vague
What earthing philosophy do you apply to signal cable shields, and how do you verify compliance on site?
02 QA/QC Process and Documentation Discipline High Weight
Provide a sample ITR (Inspection and Test Record) from a recent comparable project. Red flag if they can't or won't
How do you manage punch list items on site — what system, and who owns closure tracking?
What is your process for closing a punch item — specifically, what evidence is required before a status changes to "closed"?
How do you handle a situation where a technician marks an item complete but the supervisor is not satisfied with the quality of the work?
03 Relevant Project References — Verified High Weight
Provide two references from comparable E&I commissioning projects — specifically the client-side or PMC contact, not internal project staff. Call the reference, not email
On your most recent comparable project, what was the punch list closure rate at the time of handover? Were any items re-opened by the client PMC after initial closure?
Were there any schedule overruns attributable to E&I scope on that project? What was the cause?
04 Resource Availability and Mobilisation Realism Medium Weight
What other projects are currently active for your company, and what is the overlap with the proposed mobilisation window for this scope? Red flag if proposed supervisor is on another active project
If the project is delayed by four weeks at the start, can you hold the proposed crew? What is the cost of the delay to you, and how would you manage it?
What is your plan if a key technician is unavailable mid-project — what is the backup, and what is the handover process for in-progress work?
05 HSE Competence for the Specific Environment Medium Weight
What certifications does your proposed crew hold for this environment — offshore survival, ATEX/IECEx awareness, hot work permits, confined space entry as applicable?
Describe your process for issuing and controlling an electrical isolation permit for a 6.6kV motor circuit in a live substation environment.
What is your LTI record for the past three years, and can you provide documentation? Verify — don't accept verbal only

"We asked every subcontractor the same loop test diagnostic question. The answers ranged from a precise, systematic walkthrough to 'we would check the instrument.' That single question told us more than the entire tender document."

The Red Flags That Should Stop the Process

// Disqualifying Red Flags — Non-Negotiable
  • Unable or unwilling to provide sample ITR / test records from a previous project
  • Proposed supervisor cannot be identified by name prior to award — "we'll assign someone suitable"
  • References provided are internal project staff, not client or PMC contacts
  • Price significantly below market with no credible explanation — indicates crew quality or resource availability problem
  • Vague or incorrect answer to a basic technical scenario question from the evaluation team
  • No named individual responsible for QA/QC in the proposed organisation chart
  • Proposed crew is currently committed to another project with overlapping schedule

Integrating Evaluation Into the Award Decision

The evaluation framework above is most useful when it is applied before commercial negotiation begins — not as a post-award formality. The common failure mode is to select on price first and then attempt to verify capability, which reverses the logic and creates implicit pressure to overlook red flags in order to protect a commercial decision already made.

The correct sequence is capability screening first, reference verification second, commercial negotiation third. A subcontractor who fails the capability screen should not receive a tender — not because they are disqualified from future work, but because inviting an unqualified subcontractor to tender wastes their time and yours, and creates the possibility of a low-price bid that is difficult to reject on commercial grounds despite technical concerns.

// The One Question That Saves the Most Time

If you can only ask one question in a pre-award interview, ask for a sample inspection record from a comparable completed project — a real document, not a template. The quality, completeness, and navigability of that document is a direct proxy for the documentation discipline of the team that produced it. It takes thirty seconds to evaluate and tells you more than an hour of conversation.
