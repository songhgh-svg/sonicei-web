Hazardous Area Classification: The Gap Between Drawing and Reality

The zone classification on the P&ID is rarely what you find on the ground. We've documented the same three discrepancies across fourteen petrochemical sites in Vietnam. Here's what to audit before you energize anything.

Hazardous area classification is treated as a design activity. The process engineer draws zone boundaries on the area classification drawing, the EIC engineer specifies Ex-rated equipment accordingly, and the assumption is that the boundary on the drawing corresponds to what exists on site. It rarely does.

This is not a minor administrative gap. Energising Ex equipment in a zone it was not rated for — or operating standard equipment in a zone that should be classified — is an incident waiting to happen. On petrochemical sites in Vietnam, this gap is common, documented, and almost never systematically addressed before commissioning.

Three Discrepancies We Find on Every Site

Zone boundary drift during construction. Area classification drawings are issued early in the project, typically at FEED stage, based on process equipment layout at that time. During detailed design and construction, equipment is relocated, pipe racks are re-routed, drain pits are moved. The classification drawing is not always updated to reflect these changes. The result is physical zone boundaries that differ from the drawing — sometimes by several metres, sometimes in ways that place unrated electrical equipment inside a classified zone.

Unofficial penetrations through classified zone boundaries. Cable trays, conduit runs, and HVAC ducts that cross zone boundaries require specific treatment: sealed conduit entries, Ex-rated termination boxes, or maintained physical separation. During construction, subcontractors cut the most practical cable route, which often crosses a zone boundary without the required sealing or transition. These penetrations are invisible on a drawing review and only visible on a site walkdown.

Equipment substitution without Ex re-verification. During the procurement and construction phase, equipment is substituted — vendor lead times, budget revisions, client preferences. When a standard motor is substituted for an Ex-rated motor on a purchase order revision, someone should verify that the substitution maintains the required protection concept. In practice, this verification step is missed more often than it is performed.

The Audit That Should Happen Before Energisation

The hazardous area pre-energisation audit is a physical walkdown of the site against the current revision of the area classification drawing, conducted by an engineer with specific Ex certification knowledge. It is not the same as a general pre-commissioning safety walkdown, which will not have sufficient technical depth to identify Ex-specific issues.

// Hazardous Area Pre-Energisation Audit — Key Checks

Physical zone boundary verification against current area classification drawing → Ex certificate and T-class confirmation for every electrical item within classified zones → conduit and cable penetrations through zone boundaries sealed per IEC 60079-14 → Ex equipment installation inspection: cable gland type, earthing, IP rating, damage assessment → equipment substitution register reviewed against original Ex specification → ATEX/IECEx documentation package complete and on-site.

The output of this audit should be a formal gap register with each finding classified by risk level and assigned a mandatory closure date. Safety-critical findings — unrated equipment in Zone 1, unsealed penetrations through Zone 0 boundaries — must be closed before any energisation is permitted in the affected area. There is no schedule justification that overrides this requirement.

"The area classification drawing does not make a site safe. The physical installation makes a site safe. The drawing is a specification. The audit is the verification."

Who Is Responsible?

In most EPC contracts, Ex compliance is split across disciplines in a way that creates accountability gaps. The process engineer owns the classification drawing. The electrical engineer specifies the equipment. The civil and piping contractors install the physical boundaries. The E&I contractor installs the equipment. No single party owns the end-to-end verification that equipment, boundaries, and installation all align.

This is a project management gap as much as a technical one. On projects where we have been engaged as the commissioning authority, we assign explicit ownership of Ex compliance verification to a named individual and make it a formal pre-energisation hold point in the commissioning schedule. When no commissioning authority is in place, this verification falls through the gap between disciplines — and the site gets energised with defects that a two-day audit would have found.
