The Hidden Cost of Rework in E&I Commissioning

Rework doesn't appear as a line item on any budget report. It hides inside overtime charges, emergency mobilisation fees, demurrage penalties, and the slow erosion of client trust. Here's what it actually costs — and why most projects only find out at handover.

Ask any E&I project manager how much rework costs on a typical commissioning project and you'll get a number somewhere between "not much" and "we handle it under contingency." Ask them the same question six months after handover, once all the invoices are settled, and the answer changes.

Rework in E&I commissioning is structurally underreported — not because anyone is hiding it, but because it disaggregates across half a dozen cost codes before it ever surfaces as a single figure. By the time the true cost is visible, the project is already closed.

Why Rework Is So Hard to See in Real Time

The direct cost of rework — labour hours to redo a termination, replace a damaged instrument, or re-pull a cable — is relatively easy to quantify. It's the indirect cost that creates the budget surprise.

When a Category A defect is discovered during pre-commissioning, the repair itself might cost $800 USD. But the same defect, on an offshore platform, discovered after the client's PMC has signed off on system readiness, triggers a very different chain of events: re-mobilisation of specialist personnel, helicopter logistics, extended platform stay, and a schedule delay that cascades into adjacent work packages.

4.7× cost multiplier for defects found post-RFSU vs. pre-commissioning
62% of rework costs are indirect — mobilisation, delays, admin
18% of E&I project budget overruns attributable to undocumented rework

The 4.7× multiplier is not an outlier. It reflects the compounding effect of offshore logistics on what would, onshore, be a trivial repair. Every late defect discovery in an offshore or remote power plant context carries this multiplier risk.

The Six Places Rework Costs Hide

Based on field experience across Southern Vietnam power plant and offshore E&I projects, rework costs consistently appear under the following headings — none of which are labelled "rework" in the project accounts:

Cost Category How Rework Appears Visibility
Overtime labour Weekend mobilisation to close defects before walkdown Partially visible
Subcontractor variations Additional scope claims for re-work of earlier installations Usually hidden
Material wastage Cable, glands, and termination hardware re-ordered Hidden in procurement
Schedule penalty / demurrage Handover delay triggered by late punch list resolution Visible at close-out
Engineering re-work hours As-built corrections, re-issued drawings, re-testing Hidden in engineering codes
Client relationship cost Increased scrutiny, reduced trust, future bid disadvantage Never quantified
Source: SONIC E&I field analysis, offshore and power plant projects, Southern Vietnam, 2022–2025

"The repair took four hours. The paperwork dispute, re-inspection, and client sign-off took eleven days. That's where the money went — not the labour."

The Documentation Gap as Root Cause

In most cases, rework is not caused by poor craftsmanship. It's caused by inadequate first-time documentation — work that was done correctly but couldn't be proven correct when challenged. The client's inspector raises a finding. The contractor disputes it. There's no timestamped photo evidence of the original installation. A re-inspection is scheduled. Time passes.

This dynamic is especially damaging because it creates rework that is, strictly speaking, unnecessary — the installation was fine. But without evidence, "fine" is just an assertion. And in a contractual dispute at handover, assertions lose to documentation every time.

// Warning Signs Your Project Is Building Rework Exposure
  • Punch list closure based on verbal confirmation rather than photo evidence
  • Subcontractors marking items "complete" without supervisor sign-off
  • No systematic link between inspection records and as-built documentation
  • Client PMC receiving status updates via email rather than a live dashboard
  • Category B and C items being deferred routinely to "close out phase"

What Changes When Documentation Is Enforced at the Point of Work

The most effective rework prevention strategy is not more inspections — it's raising the documentation standard at the point where work is first completed. When a technician captures photo evidence of a completed installation before moving to the next task, three things happen simultaneously: the record is created, the technician performs a final self-check, and the closure is time-stamped against the work order.

This is the operational logic behind AI-Punch™, our mobile-first commissioning QA platform. The system enforces photo evidence as a prerequisite for item closure — not as bureaucracy, but as the minimum standard required to defend a closure claim against a client challenge. Items closed with timestamped, geotagged photo evidence are, in our experience, almost never successfully disputed.

// The Rework Prevention Equation

Evidence captured at point-of-work → closure claim is defensible → disputes don't escalate → rework cycle is broken. The cost of a 30-second photo is a fraction of one percent of the cost of one disputed re-inspection offshore.

An Honest Cost Estimate

On a mid-scale offshore E&I commissioning package (instrument loop testing, MCC terminations, earthing verification), a project running on spreadsheet-based punch management can expect rework-related costs — direct and indirect — to consume between 8% and 15% of the commissioning contract value. That figure drops significantly when documentation is enforced at the task level. We have seen it fall below 3% on projects where photo evidence per closure item is mandatory from day one.

The investment required to enforce that standard is modest. The return, measured in avoided demurrage, avoided re-inspection costs, and maintained client trust, is not.
