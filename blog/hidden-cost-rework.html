The Hidden Cost of Rework: A Field Engineer's View of the $100K Mistake

A 1% rework rate on a $10M E&I contract sounds acceptable. It isn't. We break down where that money actually goes — and why most project managers never see the full number.

When a project manager calculates rework cost, the number that lands in the report is usually labour hours multiplied by rate. It's a clean, defensible figure. It's also substantially wrong.

The real cost of a rework event is distributed across five or six budget lines, some of which are never categorised as rework at all. By the time a project closes and the post-mortem is written, the direct cost number looks manageable. The indirect costs have already been absorbed elsewhere — into contingency, into schedule float, into the next project's margin.

The Anatomy of a Rework Event

Take a concrete example: a cable termination defect discovered during pre-commissioning on an offshore platform. Category B — not safety-critical, but system-blocking. The termination needs to be redone on one feeder panel. Sounds simple.

One termination. Discovered late. On a modest offshore project. The visible cost is $2,600. The real cost is closer to $36,000 — and that's before factoring in the relationship damage with the client PMC, who now scrutinises every other status report twice.

Why the Full Number Never Appears

Project accounting systems are built around work packages, not failure events. When a technician sits idle for two days because a loop can't be cleared, that's booked as productive time in a different work package. When a PMC inspector is remobilised, that's often billed to a contingency line. When a schedule slip compresses the commissioning sequence and forces parallel work that creates additional errors, the causal link to the original defect is invisible by the time the invoice lands.

"The $2,600 rework report line item is the tip of the iceberg. The part that sinks you is always below the waterline."

This isn't a failure of project management sophistication. It's a structural property of how complex projects are budgeted and tracked. The costs are real; they're just distributed in ways that make them difficult to attribute correctly after the fact.

The Multiplier at Handover

Defects discovered early in commissioning cost one amount. The same defect discovered at handover costs significantly more — typically 2.4 to 3.8 times more, based on field experience across power plant and offshore projects in Southern Vietnam.

The reason is straightforward: at handover, everything is time-critical. The client's window is fixed. The platform schedule is fixed. Your ability to absorb delays without triggering penalties is near zero. A defect that would have cost three hours to fix in week four of commissioning now triggers a formal non-conformance, a client response window, a re-inspection cycle, and potentially a commercial dispute about which party bears the delay cost.

// The Handover Multiplier

A 1% rework rate on a $10M E&I contract = $100K in direct labour costs. Apply the 2.4× hidden cost multiplier: true rework exposure = $240,000+ — before demurrage, penalties, or contract dispute costs. On a project with tight handover commitments, a single bad week can erase the entire project margin.

What Changes When You Catch Defects Earlier

The answer is not "hire better technicians" or "write better procedures." Both help, but neither addresses the core problem: defects are inevitable in complex E&I work, and the cost is determined almost entirely by when you find them.

This is why the investment case for experienced QA supervision and structured inspection workflows is straightforward even at a premium day rate. If a senior E&I engineer with Siemens or GE spec experience catches a termination issue during loop testing rather than at pre-handover walkdown, the cost difference is roughly an order of magnitude.

It's also why we believe AI-assisted punch list management (the subject of our previous article) addresses a real economic problem rather than a documentation convenience. The value isn't better paperwork — it's earlier defect visibility, which directly compresses the cost multiplier.

// The Practical Implication

If your project has a $500K contingency budget and a tight handover schedule, the question isn't whether to invest in qualified QA supervision. The question is whether $500K of contingency adequately covers the tail risk of a rework cluster hitting in the final month. In most offshore projects we've reviewed: it doesn't.
