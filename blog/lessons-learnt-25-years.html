25 Years of E&I Commissioning: The Lessons That Cost the Most to Learn

After 25 years and hundreds of E&I commissioning projects across Vietnam and Southeast Asia, the patterns are clear. The mistakes that repeat. The decisions that save projects. The things nobody tells you before your first handover.

Experience in E&I commissioning is not measured in years. It is measured in handovers — in the number of times you have stood in a control room at midnight arguing over a punch list, or watched a transformer fail energisation because someone skipped a check that seemed redundant at the time. Twenty-five years of that accumulates into something that cannot be found in a commissioning manual.

What follows is not a technical checklist. It is a set of observations drawn from projects that went well, projects that did not, and the handful of moments on each that determined which category they fell into.

01

The Schedule Is a Fiction. The Readiness Is Not.

Every project has a commissioning schedule. On the majority of projects, that schedule bears almost no relationship to the sequence in which work actually becomes ready for testing. Construction hands over systems on its own timeline. Vendors arrive late. Documentation lags behind physical completion by weeks.

The commissioning team that treats the schedule as fixed will spend most of its time waiting, re-sequencing, and absorbing the frustration of a programme that was never achievable. The team that treats the schedule as a target and manages readiness as a separate, daily discipline will consistently outperform it — not because they work faster, but because they never stop on a loop that isn't ready to test.

The lesson: Build a readiness tracker from day one. Not a schedule — a tracker. Who owns each loop. What is blocking it. What the resolution date is. Update it every morning. The schedule tells you where you should be. The readiness tracker tells you where you actually are.

02

The Most Expensive Person on Site Is the One Who Doesn't Ask Questions.

Early in a career, asking questions feels like admitting a gap. In commissioning, not asking questions is how you energise a section of plant that wasn't ready, or sign off a loop test that has an unresolved deviation that no one mentioned because no one asked.

The culture of a commissioning team is set by its lead engineer in the first week on site. If the lead asks questions openly — of the construction team, of vendors, of the client — the team will do the same. If the lead performs certainty he doesn't have, the team will perform it too, and the defects will hide behind it until handover makes them visible.

"I have never seen a project fail because someone asked too many questions. I have seen several fail because the right question wasn't asked until it was too late to act on the answer."

03

Documentation Is Not Bureaucracy. It Is the Product.

The plant either runs or it doesn't — that much is visible on day one of operation. What is not visible is whether the commissioning was done correctly, whether the deviations were managed, whether the as-built condition matches what was tested. That is only visible in the documentation.

On projects where documentation was maintained rigorously throughout commissioning, handover takes days. On projects where documentation was treated as something to be compiled after the real work was done, handover takes weeks — and the team spends those weeks reconstructing records from photographs, memory, and argument.

The lesson: A loop test record completed at the time of testing, with as-found and as-left values, a DCS screenshot, and two signatures, takes four minutes. Reconstructed from memory six weeks later, it takes forty minutes and is worth a fraction of the original. Do it once, do it right, do it now.

04

The Client's PMC Is Not Your Adversary. Treat Them Like One and You Will Make Them One.

Project Management Consultants exist to protect the client's interests. That is their function. On projects where the commissioning contractor treats the PMC as an obstacle to be managed — releasing information selectively, contesting every punch item, presenting status reports designed to obscure rather than inform — the relationship deteriorates into a formal dispute process that consumes time and money on both sides.

On projects where the commissioning team invites the PMC into the process early — shares the readiness tracker, flags issues before they become punch items, presents bad news directly — the PMC typically becomes an ally in resolving the problems that will always exist on a complex commissioning project.

Tell the client about a problem before they find it themselves, and you have a problem to solve together. Let them find it first, and you have a dispute. The technical content of the problem is identical. The relationship cost is not. In 25 years, the projects that finished on time almost always had a commissioning lead who understood this distinction instinctively.

05

Rework Is Never Just the Cost of the Rework.

When a defect is found and corrected, the direct cost — labour, materials, re-testing — is visible and quantifiable. What is rarely quantified is the indirect cost: the schedule compression that follows, the overtime required to recover, the additional management attention diverted from other systems, the client confidence that does not recover even after the defect does.

The economics of prevention versus rework are not complicated. A pre-commissioning check that catches a wiring error costs one hour. The same error found at energisation costs a day of fault-finding, a system outage, a re-test, and an entry in the client's risk register against your company's name. Prevention is not a cost. It is the cheapest insurance available on a commissioning project.

06

The First Week on Site Determines the Last Week.

The final week of a commissioning project — the pre-handover push, the outstanding punch items, the documentation sprint — is almost entirely a consequence of decisions made in the first week. Whether the readiness tracking was set up correctly. Whether the documentation system was established before testing began. Whether the relationship with the PMC started on the right footing.

Teams that arrive on site and spend the first week "getting oriented" before setting up systems consistently find themselves in crisis in the final week. Teams that treat day one as the most important day of the project — setting up the tracker, meeting the PMC, walking the site with the construction lead — consistently find the final week manageable.

"You cannot recover in week twelve what you didn't build in week one. The project that finishes cleanly was planned cleanly from the first morning on site."

07

Technical Competence Gets You on the Project. Judgment Gets You the Next One.

The E&I commissioning industry in Vietnam is smaller than it appears. Reputations — of companies and of individual engineers — travel faster than any marketing effort. A commissioning lead who makes sound decisions under pressure, who manages bad news with transparency, and who delivers documentation that the client can actually use will be requested by name on the next project. One who performs competence without it will not.

The technical baseline — loop testing, MCC commissioning, instrument calibration, documentation standards — is a threshold requirement. Every credible contractor meets it, or claims to. What differentiates the work that actually gets done correctly is judgment: knowing when to stop and escalate, when to push through, when to involve the client, and when the schedule pressure is being used to justify cutting a corner that will reappear as a defect six months after handover.

◆

None of these lessons are difficult to understand. They are, in retrospect, obvious. The reason they cost so much to learn is that they are only truly understood after you have experienced the consequence of ignoring them — the midnight punch list dispute, the post-handover fault call, the client who doesn't call again.

The value of 25 years is not knowing more technical specifications. It is having developed the instinct to recognise, early enough to act on it, the moment when a project is drifting toward one of these familiar failure modes. That instinct is what we bring to every project we take on.
