Offshore E&I Is a Different Game

Offshore commissioning shares the same fundamentals as onshore work — and almost nothing else. What engineers who've only worked onshore need to understand before they step on a vessel.

The electrical and instrumentation work on an offshore vessel or platform is technically identical to what you'd find in a power plant or industrial facility ashore. The same cable terminations, the same loop testing methodology, the same IEC standards. The engineering knowledge transfers almost completely.

What doesn't transfer is everything else — and "everything else" is what determines whether a project succeeds or fails.

The Logistics Are the Job

On an onshore project, if a technician forgets a tool, they drive back to the workshop. If a spare part is needed, procurement delivers it tomorrow. If the drawings need to be updated and reprinted, someone does it in the site office overnight.

Offshore, none of those options exist. What's on the vessel is what you have. Mobilisation planning — the selection of tools, spare instruments, cable glands, terminal blocks, test equipment, and consumables — is as technically demanding as the commissioning work itself. An offshore E&I engineer who has never prepared a mobilisation pack for a vessel job will underestimate this by a factor of three, and the project will pay for it in helicopter costs or outright schedule failure.

Rotation Handover Is the Hidden Risk

On a 28/28 rotation offshore, the commissioning team changes entirely at the midpoint of most projects. The incoming team receives a handover pack — typically a progress spreadsheet and a brief verbal from the departing supervisor — and is expected to continue seamlessly.

In practice, an enormous amount of tacit knowledge walks off the vessel with the departing team. Which junction boxes have been re-identified informally during the job. Where the as-built condition of a cable tray differs from the drawing. Which instrument has a field adjustment that was never formally documented. All of that lives in people's heads, and when those people leave, the incoming team discovers it through defects.

"A well-run offshore commissioning project treats every rotation boundary as a mini-handover to a skeptical client. Everything is documented as if the next person has never been on this vessel before. Because they haven't."

Our standard practice on offshore projects includes a mandatory 48-hour overlap between rotations, a site-specific deviation log maintained daily, and photo-documented as-built updates at each loop closure. This is overhead that some clients push back on during mobilisation planning. It consistently pays back by the end of the project.

SIMOPS: The Context That Changes Everything

Simultaneous Operations — the management of multiple concurrent activities in a shared space with shared hazards — is a normal part of offshore project execution and one that onshore engineers frequently underestimate.

On a vessel undergoing E&I commissioning, you may be working alongside marine crew doing structural work, production teams managing live process lines, and other contractor groups on completely separate scopes. The hazard interactions between these activities — hot work adjacent to live hydrocarbon systems, high-voltage testing near other work groups, confined space work in cable ducts — require a level of coordination and hazard awareness that is fundamentally different from onshore PTW systems.

What to Look for When Selecting an Offshore E&I Contractor

The qualification questions that matter for offshore E&I work are different from onshore. Beyond the standard commissioning track record, ask:

Have they managed offshore rotation handovers on projects longer than one rotation? If their only offshore experience is short-duration work where the same team sees the project from mobilisation to demobilisation, they haven't been tested on the hardest part.

Do they have a mobilisation methodology? Ask to see their standard offshore tool and spares matrix for a project similar to yours. A contractor who builds this from scratch each time doesn't have the offshore experience they claim.

How do they handle documentation offshore? Paper-based drawing control is the norm — their process for managing revisions, ensuring field teams are working from current drawings, and controlling as-built markups in a bandwidth-limited environment should be clearly defined.

The technical E&I capability is table stakes. The offshore operational discipline is what actually separates contractors when the conditions get difficult — which, on a long offshore project, they always do.
