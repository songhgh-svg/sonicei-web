Pre-Commissioning Checks: The 48 Hours That Determine Your Entire Schedule

Pre-commissioning in E&I is not a formality. It is the last line of defence before energisation — and most project teams treat it like an administrative afterthought. The resulting failures are both predictable and expensive.

In the standard project schedule, pre-commissioning appears as a short phase between mechanical completion and first energisation. On paper it looks manageable: a few days, a checklist, sign-offs, then power on. In practice, it is the most information-dense 48-to-72-hour window in the entire project lifecycle. What gets missed here doesn't stay hidden — it shows up as tripped breakers, damaged equipment, or safety incidents on energisation day.

The compression of pre-commissioning time is not random. It is a predictable consequence of schedule slippage earlier in the project. Mechanical completion runs late, the client's energisation date doesn't move, and the pre-commissioning phase absorbs the delay. This is how a 72-hour verification window becomes a 16-hour one.

What Pre-Commissioning Actually Encompasses

There's a widespread misunderstanding about what pre-commissioning is. On many sites, it's treated as equivalent to installation inspection — visually confirming that equipment is physically present and appears correctly installed. That is not pre-commissioning. That is a construction close-out check, and it catches a fraction of the defects that proper pre-commissioning identifies.

True E&I pre-commissioning encompasses insulation resistance testing of all power and instrument cables, continuity verification of every control and signal circuit, correct fuse and protection relay setting confirmation against the as-built design, verification of all interlocks in de-energised state, polarity checks on CT and PT secondary circuits, and MCB/breaker trip testing against rated current values.

// Pre-Commissioning Minimum Checklist — E&I Systems

IR testing: all power cables ≥500V DC Megger, minimum 100MΩ at 1 minute. Control cable continuity: point-to-point per loop drawing. Interlock simulation: all process safety interlocks exercised in de-energised mode. Protection relay settings: confirmed against approved relay setting schedule. CT polarity: verified before any differential protection energisation. Phase rotation: confirmed at every MDB, DB, and motor terminal box.

Each of these checks requires specific test equipment, documented test sheets, and an engineer who can interpret the results against design intent — not just record a pass or fail against a generic checklist.

The Insulation Resistance Testing Problem

IR testing is the check most frequently performed incorrectly under schedule pressure. The mechanics are simple enough that it gets delegated to junior technicians without adequate supervision. The interpretation of results is where errors occur.

A cable reading 50MΩ is not automatically a fail, but it warrants investigation — particularly if adjacent cables in the same tray are reading 2,000MΩ or above. A fresh cable in a clean, dry environment should read essentially infinite resistance. A reading of 50MΩ on a new installation indicates moisture ingress, insulation damage, or incorrect termination, and should trigger a systematic fault-finding process, not a re-test and re-run on the same result.

"We energised a 11kV feeder based on a 'pass' IR test. The technician had tested the cable cores to earth. Nobody had tested core-to-core. We found out about the phase-to-phase fault the expensive way."

IR testing protocols must be explicitly defined in the project's pre-commissioning procedure: which voltage level, which measurement duration, which measurement configuration (core-to-earth, core-to-core, screen-to-earth), and what the acceptance criteria are for each cable type. If those parameters are not specified before the technician picks up the Megger, the results are not reliable.

Interlock Verification: The Most Skipped Step

Process safety interlocks — the hardwired and software-based systems that prevent equipment from operating in unsafe conditions — are the most frequently skipped element of pre-commissioning. The reason is consistent: the process systems aren't ready, so the interlocks "can't be tested yet," and the energisation schedule moves forward without them.

This is a dangerous compromise. Many interlocks can be verified in de-energised simulation mode without requiring live process conditions. A fire and gas shutdown relay can be tested with a test switch. An over-temperature trip can be simulated at the thermostat. Motor start interlocks can be traced through the control system in loop check mode. The assumption that interlock testing requires live process is often incorrect and always worth challenging before accepting schedule-driven deferrals.

The Documentation Requirement

Pre-commissioning without documentation is not pre-commissioning. It is an informal walkdown, and it provides no protection to the project, the contractor, or the client. Every IR test result, every continuity measurement, every interlock verification must be recorded on a test sheet that includes the date, the technician's name, the instrument used (with calibration status), and the specific result against the acceptance criterion.

This documentation serves two purposes: it is the quality record that supports the handover package, and it is the diagnostic baseline that the operations team uses for years afterward when tracing faults. A project that hands over with complete, accurate pre-commissioning records saves its client significant engineering time in the first year of operation.

The investment in thorough pre-commissioning — done correctly, in sufficient time, with competent supervision — is the single highest-return quality activity in the E&I project lifecycle. No other check catches as many defects, at a lower correction cost, before they become schedule-critical problems.
