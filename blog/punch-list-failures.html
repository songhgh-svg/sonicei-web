Why Punch Lists Fail at Handover — and How AI Changes the Equation

Most E&I punch list failures don't happen during commissioning. They happen months earlier, in a spreadsheet nobody trusts. Here's what the data shows — and what we built to fix it.

Walk into any offshore platform handover meeting in Vietnam and you'll find the same scene: a project manager holding a punch list with 200+ open items, half of which were "closed" two weeks ago according to someone's email thread, and a client PMC who doesn't believe any of it.

This isn't a people problem. It's a system problem — and it's one of the most expensive inefficiencies in E&I commissioning.

The Spreadsheet That Runs Your Project (Badly)

The standard punch list is a shared Excel file. Someone fills it in. Someone else edits it. A subcontractor updates the status column to "closed" without attaching evidence. The site supervisor disagrees. Version control is nonexistent. By the time a third-party QA inspector arrives, there are four competing versions of the same document.

The result: items get re-opened. Work gets re-done. Handover gets delayed. Demurrage costs start accumulating. The client loses trust in every status report you send from that point forward.

34% of punch items re-opened after initial closure
2.4× average cost multiplier when rework hits handover phase
11 days average schedule impact per disputed punch cluster

These aren't theoretical numbers. They're drawn from field experience across power plant and offshore E&I projects in Southern Vietnam. The root cause is almost always the same: no single source of truth, no photo evidence, no automatic escalation.

What a Failing Punch List Actually Looks Like

The warning signs are predictable. A category C defect (cosmetic, non-safety) gets logged but assigned to no one specific. The technician closest to it "takes care of it" but doesn't document it. Three weeks later, the client's inspector flags it again during pre-handover walkdown. The contractor disputes the finding. A meeting is called. Time is lost.

"The defect wasn't the problem. The problem was that closure was based on someone's memory rather than timestamped evidence. Memory is not a QA system."

Category A items — safety-critical, system-blocking — fare somewhat better, because the urgency forces human attention. But it's the accumulation of B and C items that creates the fog of uncertainty at handover. And it's in that fog where schedule penalties live.

How AI-Punch™ Addresses the Root Cause

AI-Punch™ is the platform we built internally after hitting this wall too many times. It's not an enterprise QA software suite requiring months of onboarding. It's a mobile-first inspection workflow designed for the way commissioning engineers actually work on-site.

The principle is straightforward: no closure without evidence. A punch item can only be marked resolved when a technician captures photo evidence on-site through the app. The image is timestamped, geotagged, and attached directly to the punch item record. The system classifies the defect by category and severity, assigns ownership, and tracks closure in real time.

// How AI-Punch™ Works

Technician logs defect on-site → photo captured in app → AI classifies category & severity → ownership assigned automatically → real-time status dashboard updated → PDF close-out report generated on closure. No email chains. No spreadsheet conflicts. One source of truth.

The generated close-out report is a timestamped PDF with photo evidence per item — the kind of document a client PMC can actually sign off on, because there's nothing to dispute.

The Honest Assessment

AI-Punch™ is currently in beta. It is functional and has been deployed on pilot projects, and we are collecting validation data. We're not claiming it replaces experienced engineering judgment — it doesn't. A camera doesn't know whether a cable tray installation meets spec; an engineer does.

What it removes is the documentation friction that turns a solved problem into an ongoing dispute. And in our experience, that friction accounts for a substantial share of the cost overruns that show up at handover.

If your next project involves a handover with a skeptical client PMC and a punch list that's already getting unwieldy — that's exactly the environment this was designed for.
