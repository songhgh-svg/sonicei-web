What a Client PMC Actually Wants at Handover

Most contractors prepare for handover. Very few prepare for the handover meeting. Understanding what a PMC needs to sign off — and why they hesitate — is the difference between a clean close and a three-week dispute that nobody budgeted for.

A Project Management Consultant hired by the client has one job at handover: protect their client from accepting a system that will cause problems after the contractor leaves. Everything they do in a handover meeting is in service of that objective. Understanding this — not as an adversarial dynamic, but as a legitimate professional function — is the starting point for a handover strategy that actually works.

The contractors who consistently achieve clean, fast handovers are not the ones who do the least-disputed work. They're the ones who make it easiest for the PMC to sign off. That distinction matters more than it might appear.

The PMC's Four Core Concerns

Behind every challenge raised in a handover meeting, one of four concerns is driving it. Recognising which concern is in play — and addressing it directly — is far more effective than defending the technical quality of the work.

01
Completeness
Is everything in scope actually done? The PMC needs evidence that the punch list is not just closed on paper but closed in fact — with documentation to prove each item.
02
Traceability
Can every test result, calibration certificate, and inspection record be traced back to a specific tag, loop, or system? If the documentation is not navigable, it doesn't function as evidence.
03
Authority
Who signed off on what, and are they authorised to do so? A closure record signed by the wrong person — or no person — creates liability exposure the PMC will not accept.
04
Operability
Will the system perform in service? The PMC needs confidence that commissioning was done to operate the plant, not just to pass a test. This is hardest to document but is addressed through test thoroughness.

Most handover disputes trace back to a failure against one of these four criteria — not to a genuine deficiency in the work itself. A system can be correctly installed and still fail handover because the traceability of that fact is inadequate.

What the PMC Is Checking When They Challenge a Closure

When a PMC inspector challenges a closed punch item, they are not necessarily claiming the work wasn't done. They are testing whether the closure can be proven. This is a subtle but important distinction, because it means the right response to a challenge is not a technical argument — it's producing the evidence.

"I don't doubt your technicians did the work. What I need is something I can attach to the close-out report that my client can file for their operational records. An email thread doesn't do that."

This is a reasonable position. The PMC is building a handover package that will live in the client's document management system for the operational life of the facility. Verbal assurances and email chains are not appropriate inputs to that package. Timestamped, signed inspection records with photo evidence are.

The Handover Documentation Package: Minimum Standard

// E&I Handover Documentation — PMC-Ready Standard
01
Punch List Close-Out RegisterPer-item record with category, description, closure date, responsible party signature, and photo evidence. No open Cat A or B items at handover date.
02
Instrument Loop Test RecordsSigned test sheets per loop tag, with as-found and as-left values, DCS confirmation, and test engineer signature. Indexed by loop number and P&ID reference.
03
Calibration Certificate DatabaseCurrent calibration certificates for every field instrument, organised by tag number, with calibration date, due date, and calibration authority.
04
Earthing & Continuity Test RecordsResistance measurements at each earth point, signed by qualified E&I engineer, cross-referenced to earthing schedule and single-line diagram.
05
Cable Megger Test RecordsInsulation resistance values per cable number, test voltage, pass/fail status, and date. Indexed against cable schedule.
06
Red-Line As-Built DrawingsMarked-up IFC drawings reflecting all field deviations. Signed and dated by responsible engineer. Formal as-built revision submitted or timeline confirmed.
07
Pre-Commissioning Completion CertificatesSystem-level certificates signed by commissioning lead and client witness, confirming completion of pre-commissioning scope per ITR (Inspection and Test Record) requirements.
08
Outstanding Items RegisterAny items deferred by agreement — with written client acceptance, agreed close-out date, and responsible party. No surprises. Agreed exceptions are acceptable; undisclosed exceptions are not.

The Relationship Dynamic That Determines Speed

Beyond documentation, the speed of handover sign-off is heavily influenced by the relationship the commissioning team has built with the PMC over the course of the project. PMCs who have received reliable, consistent status reporting throughout commissioning — and who have seen the contractor resolve issues promptly when they arose — arrive at the handover meeting in a fundamentally different frame of mind than those who have spent three months chasing updates.

3.2× faster handover sign-off on projects with live dashboard access for PMC
78% of handover disputes involve documentation issues, not technical defects
21 days average time lost to handover disputes on projects without structured close-out packages

The 3.2× figure reflects a consistent finding: when the PMC has had visibility of commissioning progress throughout the project — not just at handover — their sign-off process is dramatically faster. There are no surprises to investigate, no credibility gaps to overcome, and no reason to scrutinise documentation more carefully than necessary.

// The Handover Readiness Test

Before requesting a handover meeting, ask yourself: if the PMC's inspector spent two hours with your close-out package and nothing else — no verbal explanation, no emails, no access to your team — could they verify completeness, traceability, and authority of sign-off for every system in scope? If the answer is anything other than "yes," the package is not ready.
